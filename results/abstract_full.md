# Abstract

Physical fitness has been shown to correlate with cognitive performance across multiple domains, yet prior work has relied on static, cross-sectional associations. Manning et al. (2022) demonstrated correlations between wearable fitness metrics and cognitive scores, but their analysis did not examine temporal dynamics or test whether these relationships could support predictive models. We extend this work by asking whether temporal patterns of fitness behavior—specifically, activity trends over time—predict individual cognitive performance, and whether deep learning approaches can generalize such predictions across participants.

We analyzed 104 participants (74 training, 20 testing, 10 validation) with continuous wearable fitness data and cognitive assessments across four tasks: free recall (immediate and delayed) and spatial learning (intercept and slope). We aggregated daily fitness features into temporal summaries (mean, standard deviation, and linear trend) for 15 wearables metrics, yielding 45 features per participant. We fit Ridge regression models with cross-validated regularization and validated findings using permutation testing (n=1000) to account for our small sample size. We also attempted an LSTM sequence model as a comparison.

Ridge regression revealed statistically significant fitness-cognition associations for episodic memory tasks (free recall immediate: 98.5th percentile of null distribution, p<0.05; free recall delayed: 97.3rd percentile, p<0.05), while spatial learning tasks showed no significant signal (intercept: 55.4th percentile; slope: 84.7th percentile). Activity trend features (7-day and 30-day slopes) ranked among the top predictors, indicating that changes in physical activity matter beyond average levels. In contrast, LSTM sequence models failed to generalize (mean R²=−0.91), suggesting the fitness-cognition signal is too weak or inconsistent for deep learning at this sample size.

Our results replicate Manning et al.'s directional findings while extending them temporally: activity trajectories predict episodic memory at above-chance levels, consistent with the known effects of aerobic exercise on hippocampal function. However, effect sizes remain small (mean Ridge R²=−0.09), indicating that wearable fitness features alone are insufficient for individual-level cognitive prediction. We conclude that temporal feature engineering and permutation testing reveal real but modest fitness-memory associations, with important task-specific effects requiring larger samples or richer feature sets for practical application.
